4.1 Qualitative Analysis of Enhancement Results on EUVP Samples
================================================================

This section presents a qualitative analysis of the enhancement results obtained from the three selected EUVP samples. The samples were chosen to represent three common underwater degradation conditions: low-light appearance, greenish colour cast, and blur or low contrast. For each model, the visual comparison is organised as Input, Preprocessed, and Enhanced image triplets. Since the preprocessing stage in this project mainly includes resizing, RGB conversion, and normalization, the major visible differences appear between the Input and Enhanced images.

The purpose of this qualitative study is to examine how each model affects brightness, contrast, sharpness, colour balance, and overall scene visibility. Particular attention is given to the restoration of natural colours, reduction of underwater colour cast, and improvement in object-background separation.


4.1.1 Model 1
-------------

Model 1 represents the baseline encoder-decoder CNN architecture. The qualitative results produced by this model show that it is capable of improving the visual quality of underwater images, although the enhancement remains relatively conservative compared to the later models.

In the low-light sample, the enhanced result shows an increase in brightness, allowing the squid subject to appear more visible than in the input image. The red and orange tones in the body become more noticeable after enhancement, and the subject stands out more clearly against the dark background. However, the overall enhancement is moderate, and some dark regions remain visually heavy. This suggests that Model 1 can recover lost visibility to some extent, but its ability to restore strong local contrast is limited.

In the greenish-cast sample, the model reduces the dominant green tone and partially restores the colour of the seabed and coral-like structures. The output appears less colour-distorted than the input, and the scene becomes easier to interpret visually. Even so, a mild greenish tint is still present in the enhanced result, indicating that the model does not fully neutralize the underwater colour cast. The correction is visible, but not very aggressive.

In the blur or low-contrast sample, Model 1 improves the visibility of the shark and slightly increases contrast between the subject and the blue background. The body contour becomes somewhat clearer, and the scene looks less faded than the input image. Nevertheless, the restoration of sharpness remains modest, and the output still preserves some softness in the object boundaries.

From these observations, it can be inferred that Model 1 performs stable baseline enhancement. It improves brightness, recovers part of the lost colour information, and modestly strengthens contrast. However, the enhancement is visually restrained, and its ability to handle severe colour cast or weak local detail is lower than that of the more advanced models.


4.1.2 Model 2
-------------

Model 2 extends the baseline design by introducing a bottleneck layer, allowing the network to learn a richer intermediate feature representation before reconstruction. Qualitatively, this model produces stronger enhancement than Model 1 in terms of contrast recovery and colour emphasis.

In the low-light sample, the enhanced squid image appears brighter and more vivid than the output of Model 1. The red body region is more strongly restored, and the foreground subject gains clearer separation from the background water. The improvement in contrast is more visible, making the enhanced image appear more striking. This indicates that the added bottleneck helps the network capture stronger feature information related to luminance and structure.

In the greenish-cast sample, Model 2 performs a clearer correction of the green underwater tint. Compared with the input image, the enhanced result reveals more neutral sandy and coral-like tones in the foreground. The scene becomes visually cleaner, and the colour distortion is reduced more effectively than in Model 1. Although a green bias is still faintly visible, the correction is stronger and the recovered scene appears more natural overall.

In the blur or low-contrast sample, the shark becomes more distinguishable from the surrounding blue water, and the visual depth of the image improves. Contrast enhancement is more pronounced than in Model 1, which helps object edges appear more defined. The scene remains naturally blue, but the tonal variation across the image becomes stronger, resulting in better readability.

Overall, Model 2 demonstrates a better balance between visibility enhancement and structural preservation. Compared with Model 1, it produces stronger contrast, more noticeable colour restoration, and clearer scene separation. The qualitative results suggest that the bottleneck layer improves the network’s capacity to represent underwater features before decoding.


4.1.3 Model 3
-------------

Model 3 is the hybrid CNN-based enhancement model that combines convolutional feature extraction with a MobileViT-style bottleneck and skip connections. Among the three models, this one provides the strongest and most consistent qualitative enhancement across the selected EUVP examples.

In the low-light sample, Model 3 recovers the foreground subject with the highest visual clarity. The squid body becomes brighter, more saturated, and more sharply separated from the background. Red and orange tones are restored more strongly than in the other two models, and the dark background appears cleaner while still preserving the underwater scene appearance. This produces the most visually prominent subject restoration among the three models.

In the greenish-cast sample, the improvement is especially noticeable. The strong green colour dominance present in the input image is reduced substantially, and the enhanced result shows better recovery of sandy, beige, and brownish tones. Foreground objects become easier to distinguish, and the image appears more natural and balanced. Compared with Model 1 and Model 2, Model 3 shows the most effective correction of underwater colour cast in this example.

In the blur or low-contrast sample, Model 3 preserves the natural bluish underwater tone while increasing contrast and improving subject visibility. The shark body becomes more visually distinct, and the separation between the shark and the background water is clearer than in the previous models. Although the enhancement does not completely remove the softness of the original scene, it achieves a better balance between clarity improvement and natural underwater appearance.

These observations indicate that Model 3 performs the most complete restoration among the three networks. Its outputs show stronger colour correction, better contrast recovery, and improved subject-background separation. The model is especially effective when the degradation involves colour distortion and low visibility, suggesting that the hybrid representation helps capture both local structure and broader contextual information.


Overall Comparative Inference
-----------------------------

The qualitative comparison across the three models reveals a gradual progression in enhancement capability. Model 1 provides the baseline level of restoration and demonstrates that even a simple encoder-decoder CNN can improve underwater image visibility. Model 2 offers a stronger reconstruction by using a bottleneck representation, which improves contrast and colour recovery compared to the baseline. Model 3 delivers the most convincing qualitative results by producing better colour restoration, more balanced contrast enhancement, and clearer subject emphasis across all three degradation types.

The before-and-after visual changes can be summarized as follows:
- In the low-light sample, all models increase brightness and recover the warm colours of the squid, but Model 3 provides the strongest restoration.
- In the greenish-cast sample, all models reduce the green colour bias, with Model 3 showing the most natural colour correction.
- In the blur or low-contrast sample, all models improve object visibility, but Model 3 achieves the clearest separation while preserving the underwater tone.

Therefore, the qualitative analysis supports the quantitative findings by showing that the hybrid model produces the most visually effective enhancement among the three studied architectures.
